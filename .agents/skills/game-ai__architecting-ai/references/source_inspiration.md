# Source Inspiration

This skill is shaped by the practical game-AI tradition associated with these sources and themes. Do not quote or cite these automatically in user-facing design output unless the user asks for sources.

## Books and collections

- Ian Millington — AI for Games: broad engineering decomposition of game AI.
- Steve Rabin, ed. — Game AI Pro series: practitioner-oriented shipping patterns.
- Game AI Pro 360: topic-organized entry point into Game AI Pro material.
- Dave Mark — Behavioral Mathematics for Game AI: utility AI, response curves, and decision modeling.
- Mat Buckland — Programming Game AI by Example: implementation-first FSM, steering, and goal-oriented agents.
- Robert Nystrom — Game Programming Patterns: update loops, commands, observers, components, and runtime architecture.
- Yannakakis & Togelius — Artificial Intelligence and Games: research-facing map of AI and games.
- AI Game Programming Wisdom series: older but historically important practitioner material, including GOAP-era work.

## Practitioner themes

- Game AI should optimize player experience, not abstract intelligence.
- Architecture choice is contextual.
- Good world queries and spatial primitives often matter more than the decision architecture.
- Utility AI is strong for continuous tradeoffs.
- BTs are strong for readable sequencing and fallback.
- GOAP/HTN help when action/task combinations become hard to author manually.
- Influence maps and tactical position selection are central when location choice matters.
- Director systems often create perceived intelligence through pacing, spawning, and pressure.
- Testing, debug visualization, telemetry, and deterministic replay are part of the AI, not afterthoughts.
- LLMs are best treated as authoring/spec/testing assistants by default; runtime behavior should usually remain deterministic and bounded.
