# Commons Governance and Player Law

Use this when designing shared resources, contested zones, public infrastructure, cities, markets, gathering nodes, territory, guild commons, or player-run districts.

## Core prior rewrite

Bad default: choose public, private, or instanced.

Replacement: scarce/shared resources need governance. Decide boundaries, rights, monitoring, rule modification, sanctions, arbitration, and nested authority.

## Governance pattern

| Principle | MMO translation |
|---|---|
| clear boundaries | who has access, ownership, use rights, extraction rights |
| local rule fit | rules vary by district, biome, profession, faction, event |
| collective choice | affected players can propose/ratify local rules |
| monitoring | use, depletion, abuse, and contribution are visible |
| graduated sanctions | warning → cost → suspension → exile, not instant nuclear punishment |
| conflict resolution | fast local arbitration before operator escalation |
| recognition of rights | game law recognizes player-made governance where appropriate |
| nested governance | household → squad → guild → district → coalition → world law |

## Design questions

1. What can be depleted, blocked, captured, overused, or griefed?
2. Who monitors the resource?
3. Who benefits from restraint?
4. What local norms should the system support instead of overwriting?
5. What sanction ladder exists?
6. When does operator law override player law?
7. What prevents whales, bots, or coalitions from privatizing the commons?

## Anti-patterns

- Infinite instancing that removes social negotiation.
- Scarcity without governance.
- Player law with no enforcement surface.
- Enforcement with no due process or repair.
- Operator-only governance for every local dispute.
