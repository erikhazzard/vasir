# EVE Online Lessons

Use this when the design problem involves sovereignty, coalition politics, territorial control, staging, logistics, diplomacy, labor, cartographic intelligence, or war memory.

## Core Principle

Large-scale political worlds are not held together by combat alone. They are held together by **logistics, labor, geography, treaties, intelligence, and public memory**.

## What EVE adds that theme-park MMO doctrine underweights

| Primitive | Design meaning |
|---|---|
| Sovereignty | Territory should require upkeep, logistics, and coalition maintenance, not just victory conditions. |
| Diplomacy | Alliances, pacts, neutrality, enemies, and betrayals need visibility and durable consequences. |
| Logistics | Supply lines, staging, hauling, fuel, industrial throughput, and distance are political. |
| Cartography | Chokepoints, staging routes, intel channels, and map annotations are command infrastructure. |
| Labor | Industrialists, haulers, scouts, diplomats, quartermasters, propagandists, and intelligence operators matter as much as line combatants. |
| Trust and betrayal | Espionage, infiltration, and defectors become meaningful when institutions and supply are real. |
| Memorialization | Battles matter more when their traces survive as places, records, propaganda, and monuments. |

## Design rules

### 1. Territory must be maintained, not merely won

If holding land has no upkeep, route burden, labor dependency, or diplomatic consequence, sovereignty is shallow.

Ask:

- what must be moved to keep this territory functional?
- what routes are worth defending?
- what does distance change?
- what happens when a coalition overextends?

### 2. Coalition design is a real system, not guild chat at scale

A massive world needs intermediate political layers:

- squad / crew,
- guild / corporation,
- alliance,
- coalition,
- treaty network,
- neutral or public infrastructure.

Do not collapse every large-scale relationship into “same faction” or “same guild.”

### 3. Geography must matter politically

Political geography needs:

- chokepoints,
- staging areas,
- frontier zones,
- supply corridors,
- industrial heartlands,
- vulnerable backlines,
- symbolically important locations.

If every route is equivalent, sovereignty becomes abstract spreadsheet ownership.

### 4. War should create records, not just kill counts

A political world needs:

- conflict chronologies,
- captured territory history,
- treaty history,
- major-battle memorials,
- map overlays of past campaigns,
- institutional honors and scars.

Players should be able to say not only “we fought there,” but “that place still proves it.”

### 5. Noncombat labor must be socially legible

If only combat shows up in history, the world lies about what power depends on.

Surface the work of:

- haulers,
- builders,
- crafters,
- scouts,
- intelligence operators,
- diplomats,
- recruiters,
- quartermasters,
- infrastructure maintainers.

### 6. Betrayal should be meaningful, not omnipresent noise

Espionage and defection are interesting only when trust institutions matter. If betrayal is cheap, universal, or consequence-free, it becomes ambient cynicism.

## Translation for a no-server world

| EVE-like lesson | Translation |
|---|---|
| Alliances and coalitions structure world politics. | Add formal multi-group relationships, public treaty states, coalition tools, and cross-group objectives. |
| Geography and routes govern power. | Use travel time, supply friction, staging, and chokepoints to create real political geography. |
| Industrial and logistics labor matters. | Make noncombat world labor visible and historically legible. |
| Large wars become historical landmarks. | Promote major conflicts into world records, memorials, altered routes, or commemorative geography. |
| Diplomacy is gameplay. | Give players surfaces to negotiate, signal, archive, and break agreements. |

## Failure modes

- sovereignty without maintenance;
- factions without diplomacy;
- war without logistics;
- giant fights with no historical residue;
- political play hidden in external tools;
- supply labor invisible behind combat spectacle.

## Metrics

- territory turnover plus hold duration;
- staging-point usage;
- logistics participation and route volume;
- treaty creation, duration, and breakage;
- coalition reformation after major conflict;
- map/intel layer usage;
- memorial or war-record views;
- noncombat contribution in territorial outcomes.
