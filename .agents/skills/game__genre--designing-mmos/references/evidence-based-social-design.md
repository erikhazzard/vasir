# Evidence-Based Social Design

Use this when designing communities, guilds, factions, venues, creator groups, districts, or any system where players must join, contribute, and remain attached.

## Core prior rewrite

Bad default: assume “community” appears if players can chat, group, or share a space.

Replacement: design explicit attachment, contribution, newcomer integration, governance, and commitment surfaces. Community retention depends on both common identity and common bond.

## Two attachment modes

| Attachment | Player returns because | Design surfaces | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common identity | “I am one of us.” | factions, districts, professions, world causes, rituals, symbols, titles, shared myths | shallow nationalism, exclusion, propaganda without ties |
| Common bond | “My people are here.” | squads, statics, friend cohorts, regular venues, mentorship pairs, small groups | brittle friend-group churn, cliques |

Durable MMOs need both. Bond without identity collapses when friends leave. Identity without bond becomes fandom without obligation.

## Newcomer integration ladder

```text
visitor → recognized visitor → low-risk contributor → regular → trusted member → role holder → steward → elder/alumni
```

Each step needs a visible action and a recognition surface.

| Step | System support |
|---|---|
| visitor | low-pressure entry, norms visible, purpose tags |
| recognized visitor | greetings, repeat-contact surface, lightweight thanks |
| low-risk contributor | peripheral roles, small tasks, low blame |
| regular | calendar, cohort, role identity, visible history |
| trusted member | permissions, vouches, responsibilities |
| steward | delegation, moderation, teaching, governance |
| elder/alumni | return rituals, archives, advising role |

## Contribution design

Players contribute when:
- contribution is useful;
- contribution is visible;
- contribution is not socially dangerous;
- contribution builds identity;
- contribution creates reciprocity;
- contribution leads to status or trust without turning into unpaid labor.

## Design questions

1. What common identity does this system create?
2. What common bonds does it deepen?
3. How does a newcomer become useful within 10 minutes?
4. Who greets, teaches, notices, and remembers newcomers?
5. What makes contribution feel like belonging rather than labor extraction?
6. What prevents elite cores from hardening into inaccessible cliques?
7. What alumni path exists for players who leave and later return?

## Anti-patterns

- **Chatbox community**: communication exists but contribution and attachment do not.
- **Recruitment without integration**: players join the guild but never join a subgroup.
- **Identity without work**: faction flavor exists but no shared sacrifice or civic act.
- **Bond fragility**: a whole retention loop depends on two friends staying active.
- **Invisible contribution**: helpful players create value but the institution forgets.
