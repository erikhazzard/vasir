# Motivation and Need Satisfaction

Use this reference when a design review needs to explain why players return voluntarily rather than because the game traps them.

## Core prior rewrite

Bad default: optimize engagement by adding goals, dailies, streaks, currency sinks, and progression pressure.

Replacement: design for durable satisfaction of autonomy, competence, relatedness, identity, ownership, and meaning. Engagement is evidence only if players feel more alive, capable, connected, and self-directed after play.

## Research spine

- Self-Determination Theory and PENS apply autonomy, competence, and relatedness to games. Ryan, Rigby, and Przybylski’s work frames successful games as intrinsically motivating when play satisfies psychological needs.
- The PENS model adds a games-specific measurement lens around need satisfaction in play.
- Motivation research distinguishes wanting-to-play from having-to-play. This matters for MMOs because the same hours played can reflect healthy identity or coercive avoidance.

## Design translation

| Need | MMO design question | Strong design | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Autonomy | What meaningful choice does the player own? | multiple legitimate paths, build/role intent, self-authored goals, opt-in obligations | chore compliance, fake choice |
| Competence | What does the player get better at and see clearly? | readable feedback, mastery ladders, performance clarity, learnable roles | opaque failure, external guide dependency |
| Relatedness | Who notices, needs, or remembers the player? | squads, repeated weak ties, recognition surfaces, institutions | anonymous throughput |
| Identity | Who can the player become? | professions, titles, cosmetics, homes, public roles, authored history | interchangeable avatar |
| Ownership | What is mine/ours here? | homes, maps, crafted goods, territory, institutions, memories | rented content treadmill |
| Meaning | What mattered beyond reward? | sacrifice, rescue, public record, ritual, story, civic outcome | reward-only consumption |

## Feature review questions

1. What need does this feature primarily satisfy?
2. What need does it frustrate?
3. Is the player returning because they want to, because people matter to them, or because they fear falling behind?
4. Does the feature make a player feel more capable or merely more busy?
5. Does progression widen identity or narrow the player into a treadmill?
6. Does personalization increase autonomy without breaking shared public meaning?

## Retention law

Retention is strongest when the player can say:

```text
I chose this.
I am improving.
My people know me.
This place contains part of me.
What happened here mattered.
I can leave and still return cleanly.
```

## Watchouts

- Need satisfaction can produce either healthy or unhealthy intensity depending on the rest of life context and game pressure.
- “High engagement” is not proof of good design. Pair engagement metrics with sentiment, return-after-break, shame/fear signals, and social health.
- Autonomy is not limitless freedom. It is meaningful self-direction inside coherent world law.
