# RuneScape Classic Lessons

Use this when the feature touches old-school MMO friction, skilling, banks, direct trade, market identity, wilderness-like risk, rare items, grind loops, profession identity, or low-fidelity place memory.

## Core Thesis

RuneScape Classic is not the modern production bar. It is a primitive social-density lab. It shows that a tiny, low-fidelity world can create durable memory when repeated loops concentrate players around useful places, professions, item flows, danger thresholds, and visible trade.

Default instinct: **translate the memory-producing function, not the obsolete friction.**

## Why RSC Belongs

FFXIV is strong at social expression and returnability. WoW is strong at group execution and endgame/information ecosystems. RuneScape Classic is strong at primitive locality:

```text
small map
+ low fidelity
+ inventory pressure
+ direct trade
+ resource routes
+ bank hubs
+ certification friction
+ skilling identity
+ legible risk frontiers
+ mythic scarcity
= places, professions, prices, routes, and items people remembered
```

A massive single-world MMO needs RSC's locality lessons even more than a small game did, because global scale otherwise dissolves everyone into strangers.

## Translation Table

| RSC pattern | What it created | Modern single-world translation | Do not copy blindly |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bank-standing | Players gathered where storage, risk recovery, trade, duels, and route resets overlapped. | Make storage/crafting/trade nodes into stages: visible services, commission lanes, seller identity, inspection, nearby routes, and regulars. | Do not force players to idle because the UI is bad. |
| Certificates / certing | Bulk trading had place, NPCs, terminology, and merchant friction; high-volume goods became social objects. | Add live commission boards, bulk-contract rituals, profession hubs, courier roles, and visible seller history. | Do not recreate inventory pain as virtue. |
| Direct trade | Price discovery, merchant identity, bargaining, and reputation existed in public view. | Provide bounded live-market channels, offer boards, trade reputation, and opt-in negotiation spaces beside efficient markets. | Do not let global chat become the auction house. |
| Skilling loops | Miners, fishers, woodcutters, smiths, cooks, and merchants became identities through repeated visible routes. | Design profession neighborhoods, public work orders, resource caravans, group skilling bonuses, and reasons for experts to be seen producing. | Do not turn every skill into private instanced spreadsheet progress. |
| Resource/bank distance | Logistics created route memory and economic roles. | Use travel/logistics only where it creates planning, escorts, couriers, contracts, or place identity. | Do not add dead travel. |
| Wilderness edge | A spatially legible danger threshold created staging, scouting, fear, bragging rights, and loss stories. | Build opt-in risk gradients with clear stakes, escape windows, staging zones, scouts, leader calls, and recoverable lessons. | Do not surprise players with hidden risk or unclear loss. |
| Three-round combat commitment | Combat had commitment windows and escape drama. | Use readable commitment/escape windows for PvP and high-risk PvE. | Do not copy sluggishness or unresponsive controls. |
| Fatigue / sleep words | Anti-automation entered the core loop and became a player tax. | Protect economic trust through system design, anomaly telemetry, rate design, and market structure. | Do not punish honest play as an anti-bot ritual. |
| Holiday rares | One-time scarcity became myth, status, and permanent wealth distortion. | Decide legacy-item policy before launch: commemorative, cosmetic, tradeable, bound, reissued, or museumized. | Do not accidentally create irreversible oligarchy. |
| Low-fidelity world | Simple visuals made names, routes, items, and places carry more memory. | Invest in landmark clarity, repeated loops, readable silhouettes, and named routes. | Do not assume graphical spectacle creates culture. |

## RSC-Derived Patterns

### Bank as Social Machine

A bank is not storage. It is where risk recovery, trade, route reset, identity display, and repeated contact can overlap.

Design knobs:

- visible crafting and commission surfaces;
- trade lanes with bounded chatter;
- seller/crafter identity;
- recent trade memory;
- nearby resource routes;
- duel/spar/staging space;
- route boards and local jobs;
- bank-adjacent event starts.

Diagnostic question: **after the player finishes the transaction, is there still a reason to notice another human?**

### Certificate Friction as Place-Making

RSC certificates made bulk trade inefficient, but the inefficiency had a side effect: high-volume goods had places, people, and rituals.

Modernization:

```text
manual certing → bulk contracts
cert stall → profession exchange
known merchant → seller/crafter reputation
standing around yelling prices → bounded live offer board
inventory hauling → courier/logistics contracts where meaningful
```

Diagnostic question: **what economic action creates a remembered person or place?**

### Profession Loop Identity

A skill becomes social when its production is visible, repeated, and useful to others.

Design knobs:

- public resource routes;
- expert workstations;
- localized bonuses;
- visible production history;
- work orders;
- group gathering/crafting events;
- service titles;
- profession-specific emotes/cosmetics;
- reasons for high-level players to return to lower-tier routes.

Diagnostic question: **where does a player become known as a fisher, miner, smith, cook, merchant, scout, or courier?**

### Risk-Gradient Frontier

RSC Wilderness was powerful because danger had geography. Players crossed a line and knew the moral physics changed.

Design knobs:

- visible threshold;
- bank/staging edge;
- escalating risk bands;
- stake preview;
- scouting and warning tools;
- escape/commitment windows;
- leader pings;
- loss explanation;
- recovery routes;
- trophies that preserve risk memory.

Diagnostic question: **can players tell when they opted into danger, what they risk, and how to learn from loss?**

### Scarcity Myth

A rare item becomes culture when players can point to when, where, and why it existed. Scarcity becomes poisonous when it accidentally becomes permanent class power or purchased legitimacy.

Design knobs:

- commemorative vs power-bearing distinction;
- tradeable vs bound decision;
- reissue policy;
- date-stamped provenance;
- museum/archive treatment;
- cosmetic separation from combat readiness;
- explicit legacy language.

Diagnostic question: **will this item become a story, a market distortion, or both?**

### Primitive Visibility

RSC's limited presentation made function obvious: players, banks, items, and routes mattered because there was less visual noise.

Modernization:

- reduce effect noise in high-density zones;
- make useful players legible;
- make routes named and callable;
- show profession state clearly;
- make local market roles visible;
- favor memorable silhouettes over cosmetic soup.

Diagnostic question: **if the art pass were removed, would the social loop still be legible?**

## RSC Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| Nostalgia friction | Copies pain while missing the social function. | Identify what the friction made players notice, need, or remember; preserve that only. |
| Total auction abstraction | Removes bargaining, merchant identity, market places, and price talk. | Pair efficient markets with live commissions, seller history, profession hubs, and bounded negotiation. |
| Private skilling spreadsheet | Turns professions into isolated XP bars. | Put production, routing, and service into shared places. |
| Hidden or surprise risk | Creates resentment, not frontier memory. | Use legible thresholds, stake previews, staging zones, and recovery paths. |
| Anti-automation tax on humans | Honest players feel punished; automation adapts. | Protect economic trust without interrupting the core loop. |
| Accidental forever rares | Early mistakes become permanent wealth/status distortion. | Decide legacy scarcity policy before launch. |

## RSC Feature Review Prompts

- What is the Varrock/Draynor bank equivalent: the place where storage, trade, route reset, and social identity overlap?
- What is the cert equivalent: the economic ritual that creates a remembered merchant, not just a completed transaction?
- What is the Wilderness edge equivalent: the line where stakes change and stories begin?
- What is the skilling identity loop: where does noncombat labor become visible, useful, and prestigious?
- What is the rare-item policy: which scarcity becomes myth, and which scarcity becomes poison?
- What is the fatigue scar: which anti-automation idea is really a tax on legitimate players?
- What is the modern convenience replacement: how do we keep social memory while removing old pain?

## Application to a Massive Single-World MMO

| Massive-world problem | RSC-informed fix |
|---|---|
| Infinite strangers | Repeated profession routes, local trade hubs, bank-stage neighborhoods, event cohorts, leader networks. |
| Global market flattening | Seller identity, live commissions, bounded trade channels, regional logistics, crafters with reputation. |
| Empty spectacle | Smaller repeated loops inside large terrain; named landmarks; local jobs; visible useful players. |
| Noncombat invisibility | Public production, profession titles, service histories, route mastery, visible contracts. |
| Risk feels arbitrary | Spatial thresholds, stake clarity, scouting, staging, commitment/escape windows. |
| Progression feels private | Shared work orders, group skilling, social unlocks, local resource pressure, mentor-profession loops. |

## One-Line Rule

RuneScape Classic's lesson is not “make the game inconvenient.” It is: **concentrated, repeated, useful friction can turn places, professions, items, and risk into memory.**
