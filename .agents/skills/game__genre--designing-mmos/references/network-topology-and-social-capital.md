# Network Topology and Social Capital

Use this when the design problem involves weak ties, strong ties, guilds, neighborhoods, third places, discovery, trust, churn, or community health.

## Core Principle

Good MMO social architecture designs **both**:

- **closure** for trust, norms, coordinated play, and belonging;
- **brokerage** for discovery, mobility, mentorship, diplomacy, recruiting, and cultural diffusion.

A world with only closure cliques up. A world with only brokerage becomes socially shallow.

## Terms that matter

| Term | Meaning | What it buys |
|---|---|---|
| Closure | Dense, repeated connections inside a group. | Trust, accountability, coordinated execution, emotional resilience. |
| Brokerage | Ties across otherwise disconnected groups. | Information flow, recruiting, diplomacy, discovery, market liquidity, innovation. |
| Weak ties | Lower-commitment repeated relationships. | Preferred strangers, public solo feeders, social permeability. |
| Strong ties | Durable trust relationships. | Squads, statics, crews, core guild groups. |
| Third place | A social environment people revisit that is not purely instrumental. | Regulars, light affiliation, incidental contact, cultural identity. |

## Design rules

### 1. Every major system should name its closure surface

Examples:

- squad,
- static,
- work crew,
- neighborhood circle,
- craft collective,
- venue regulars,
- raid team.

Ask: where does a player become part of a durable “we”?

### 2. Every major system should name its brokerage surface

Examples:

- market,
- public event,
- map/intel exchange,
- mentoring queue,
- festival,
- public work board,
- diplomacy layer,
- profession district.

Ask: where does a player meet the next useful person outside their current group?

### 3. Third places must do more than route traffic

A true third place supports:

- repeated co-presence,
- low-stakes interaction,
- social visibility,
- regulars,
- mixed motives,
- easy re-entry.

Banks, plazas, taverns, docks, crafting lanes, district squares, and staging camps can all be third places if they are not purely transactional.

### 4. Avoid closure traps

Too much closure creates:

- clique capture,
- recruitment bottlenecks,
- brittle succession,
- faction echo chambers,
- hard social walls for newcomers.

### 5. Avoid brokerage-only drift

Too much brokerage creates:

- infinite acquaintances,
- no durable accountability,
- shallow social memory,
- easy abandonment,
- “nice but disposable” strangers.

## Design translations

| Problem | Topology fix |
|---|---|
| Players see lots of people but remember no one | Add recognition surfaces and repeat-contact loops. |
| Guilds are strong but world culture is weak | Add inter-guild markets, festivals, diplomacy, public works, and shared fronts. |
| New players cannot penetrate veteran groups | Add mentorship, mixed-skill public goals, and weak-tie escalation paths. |
| World is all queue, no place | Add third places with regulars, roles, and bounded chatter. |
| Large groups fracture when one subgroup leaves | Add overlapping weak ties, cross-squad rituals, and multi-group infrastructure. |

## Metrics

- repeat-contact rate before friending/guilding;
- ratio of players with repeated weak ties;
- subgroup overlap inside guilds;
- cross-group event participation;
- third-place dwell after utility completion;
- mentor or broker influence on later group joining;
- closure density vs churn after leader loss.

## Anti-patterns

- **traffic masquerading as place**;
- **guild chat mistaken for society**;
- **strong ties with no bridges**;
- **matchmaking with no weak-tie layer**.
