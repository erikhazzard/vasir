# MMO Calibration

Use this to keep the quality bar high. FFXIV and WoW are not perfect templates; they are calibration anchors because they have supported large-scale, long-lived MMO cultures with multiple overlapping products. RuneScape Classic is a primitive social-density lab for skilling identity, bank-standing, direct trade, risk frontiers, and low-fidelity place memory. Classic EverQuest is a dependency-locality lab for player services, camps, corpse recovery, class asymmetry, faction geography, public-dungeon etiquette, and Luclin-style social substitution.

## Benchmark Rule

Ask whether a system is legible, resilient, and socially complete enough to stand beside FFXIV/WoW-caliber systems. Then ask the RuneScape Classic question: **would this system still create remembered places, professions, trades, routes, risk thresholds, or items if the spectacle were stripped away?**

If the answer dismisses RSC as mere nostalgia, the review is missing primitive social-density insight. If the answer copies RSC friction directly, the review is nostalgia-poisoned.

## FFXIV Calibration

FFXIV sets a high bar for:

- returnability and patch reentry;
- story as shared emotional memory;
- social legibility outside combat: glamour, housing, venues, portraits, emotes, weddings, Free Companies, statics, RP identity;
- low-pressure group entry through duties, roulettes, commendations, mentor surfaces, and story-driven content;
- player-run culture around venues, housing, external coordination, events, Free Companies, and rituals.

Questions:

- Can a player become known outside combat performance?
- Does this create fashion, housing, hosting, roleplay, mentorship, story memory, or institution identity?
- Can a returning player understand what changed and rejoin friends without humiliation?
- Does story or onboarding teach the emotional expectations the system later enforces?

## WoW Calibration

WoW sets a high bar for:

- mass-appeal coexistence of solo play, leveling, guilds, dungeons, raids, PvP, economy, and endgame aspiration;
- group execution through roles, timers, threat, positioning, loot, leadership, logs, and preparation;
- guild ecology at scale, including small trust groups, large redundancy groups, raid teams, social guilds, and failure patterns;
- aspirational vertical progression through gear, titles, mounts, logs, raid prestige, and visible achievement;
- information ecosystems: add-ons, logs, guides, sims, videos, wikis, community tools, and data culture.

Questions:

- Is the group-execution layer readable under repeated dungeon/raid pressure?
- Are roles, loot rules, logs, and leadership expectations legible before failure?
- Does progression create aspiration without permanently exiling friends?
- Does the institution layer support both small squads and large identity shells?
- Has the information policy decided what belongs in base UI, advanced UI, logs, APIs, and hidden mystery?

## RuneScape Classic Calibration

RuneScape Classic is not the modern quality bar for UX, scale, accessibility, or production polish. It is the calibration anchor for primitive locality and profession memory.

RSC sets a high signal bar for:

- skilling identity: noncombat labor can become a public persona;
- bank-standing: storage and route reset can become social infrastructure;
- direct trade: markets can produce merchants, price talk, and reputation;
- certificates: bulk exchange friction can create places and rituals around high-volume goods;
- wilderness risk: spatially legible danger can create staging, scouting, fear, loss stories, and bragging rights;
- rare items: one-time scarcity can become myth and long-term status distortion;
- low-fidelity place memory: tiny repeated loops can beat expensive spectacle for social recall;
- fatigue as scar: anti-automation mechanics that interrupt honest play become resentment and arms-race theater.

Questions:

- Where does noncombat production become visible enough to create a known profession?
- Which bank, exchange, route, or resource node becomes a place players remember?
- What economic ritual creates merchant identity without forcing obsolete tedium?
- What is the legible risk threshold where players knowingly cross into danger?
- Which rare items are allowed to become myth, and which would poison status trust?
- Which inconveniences create relationships, and which only create clicking?

## Single-World Translation

FFXIV, WoW, and RSC all contain lessons shaped by older topology, scale, UI, and community constraints. For a massive no-server world, translate the lesson rather than copying topology or friction.

| Legacy lesson | Single-world translation |
|---|---|
| Realm identity creates bounded memory. | Create locality through neighborhoods, regions, cohorts, leaders, squads, routes, venues, professions, markets, and recurring objectives. |
| Server reputation constrains behavior. | Use institution, leader, recent-ally, market, profession, and event-front reputation surfaces. |
| Cross-server queue improves population. | In a single world, population is available; the challenge is expectation matching and memory retention. |
| Data-center travel changes social reach. | In one world, the issue is preserving local meaning inside global reach. |
| RSC banks/certs created trade places. | Create profession hubs, commissions, seller identity, and bounded live trade surfaces without copying manual cert pain. |
| RSC Wilderness created risk memory. | Create spatially legible risk gradients, staging edges, stake clarity, and recovery lessons. |
| RSC skilling made professions visible. | Put production loops, resource routes, and service histories into public space. |

## Warhammer / Failure-Scar Rule

Warhammer-like examples may be useful for open-warband authority ambiguity, hybrid role coercion, public coordination friction, memorable PvP terrain, faction texture, visible economy/spam pollution, and social friction around leader tools.

But do not use them as the quality bar. Translate any useful scar upward:

```text
anecdote → failure pattern → durable design requirement → measurable signal
```

## Source Spine to Preserve for Later Verification

- WoW ambient sociality, public solo play, and “alone together” dynamics: Ducheneaut, Yee, Nickell, Moore.
- WoW mass appeal, class popularity, solo-friendly leveling, and endgame split: Nick Yee.
- WoW guild ecology and guild failure patterns: Ducheneaut/Yee/Nickell/Moore and related guild studies.
- WoW raiding as timers, meters, add-ons, logs, and knowledge work: Golub.
- FFXIV RP, housing, external coordination, venue culture, and player-run social infrastructure: Tate and related FFXIV social research.
- FFXIV quest-system evolution and catch-up/patch structure: Wang et al. and related analysis.
- FFXIV Tam-Tara/Edda as narrative modeling of party ethics: Wong and related narrative analysis.
- FFXIV loot fairness across progression/farm contexts: Bridger and related loot/social fairness work.
- RuneScape Classic as primitive locality, skilling identity, bank-standing, direct trade/certs, Wilderness risk, rare-item myth, and anti-automation scar.
- Bartle as pressure map, not personality taxonomy.
- Yee motivation model as motive-stack map, not rigid player type system.
- Warhammer examples as failure scars only, never benchmark authority.

## Classic EverQuest Calibration Addendum

Classic EQ is not the modern UX bar. It is the dependency-memory bar: it made specific players useful through danger, class asymmetry, travel, camps, corpse recovery, and player-created markets.

| Classic EQ signal | Design question |
|---|---|
| Player services | Which players become infrastructure: porter, buffer, rezzer, tracker, scout, guide, puller, controller, crafter, merchant? |
| Corpse/recovery | Does danger create rescue, gratitude, and retry, or destructive gear/XP hostage pain? |
| Camps/public dungeons | What named camp, route, pull, spawn, or etiquette survives the session? |
| Downtime rhythm | Is the pause a social recovery/planning beat or dead waiting? |
| Class asymmetry | Are useful roles deeper than tank/healer/DPS? |
| Faction geography | Does place identity affect belonging, access, disguise, escort, and reputation? |
| EC/GFay market memory | Does the market create merchant identity and price conversation, or only search rows? |

## Luclin Calibration Addendum

Do not encode “Luclin killed EQ” as a simple historical fact. The sharper design diagnosis is:

> Luclin began replacing player-usefulness systems and provincial place memory with centralized convenience, production spectacle, and optimization treadmills faster than it supplied replacement memory.

| Luclin pattern | What improved | What was socially weakened |
|---|---|---|
| Bazaar | Search, asynchronous selling, buyer reach, time efficiency. | EC/GFay market ritual, bargaining, merchant identity, price talk. |
| Nexus/spires | Easier travel and new-content access. | Porter economy, regional gathering, overland stories, perceived world scale. |
| New models/moon layer | Production ambition, new race/class/lore. | Identity continuity and old-world texture. |
| Alternate Advancement | Post-cap goals and specialization. | Infinite optimization pressure and capability filtering. |
| Vex Thal-style gates | Epic institutional goals. | Roster burden, exclusion, and long-camp logistics debt. |

Luclin rule:

```text
convenience is allowed only with replacement memory
```

Never replace an emergent player-usefulness loop with central convenience unless the new system deliberately preserves recognition, profession identity, place memory, or repeated contact.
