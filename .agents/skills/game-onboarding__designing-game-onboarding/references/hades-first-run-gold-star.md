# Hades — S-Tier Onboarding Reference

**Genre:** roguelite action dungeon crawler  
**Reference slice:** first launch → first escape attempt → first death → first return to the House → second run setup, plus first-time Tartarus boss context  
**Why this is a gold-star reference:** Hades turns the genre’s most hostile concept — death/reset — into the central onboarding reward. The player does not merely lose and retry. The player dies, returns home, receives new dialogue, discovers permanent progression, deepens relationships, and begins again with more knowledge and often more power.

---

## Core thesis

Hades is not S-tier because it has a clean combat tutorial. Its combat tutorial is good, but not uniquely miraculous. Hades is S-tier because it teaches the player to reinterpret failure.

Most roguelites have to explain this contract:

> You will die. You will lose most of this run. But you will keep knowledge, resources, unlocks, and mastery.

Hades makes that contract diegetic.

When Zagreus dies, he does not reload from a menu. He emerges from the Pool of Styx into his father’s house. Death becomes the front door. The hub is not just a menu between attempts. It is home, prison, workplace, social circle, upgrade room, archive, antagonist space, and emotional reset.

The first escape attempt is therefore not a failed run. It is the prologue that unlocks the real game loop.

The onboarding structure is:

1. Start in motion, already rebelling.
2. Teach base combat through contained chambers.
3. Give the player a divine modifier that feels like story and build choice at the same time.
4. Let the player collect resources whose meaning is not fully clear yet.
5. Let the player die.
6. Reveal that death was not a reset, but a new form of progression.
7. Send the player back out with more knowledge, more context, and sometimes more power.

This is the Hades equivalent of Mega Man X’s Vile/Zero scene. Mega Man X says, “You are weak now, but you can become strong.” Hades says, “You died, but death is part of the route.”

---

## Scope and caveats

Hades is a roguelite. Exact room order, reward order, and first-death timing can vary. The first attempt has guardrails and restrictions, but it is not a perfectly fixed linear stage like Mega Man X’s intro highway.

The important onboarding unit is not a specific room number. It is the first loop:

**escape attempt → temporary power → resource collection → death → House return → permanent interpretation → next attempt.**

A first-time player may die in chamber 2, chamber 8, the first mini-boss, Megaera, Asphodel, or much later. The design still works because the moment of death is the onboarding reveal.

Community documentation notes several first-run restrictions: the starting weapon is the Stygian Blade, Zagreus begins without Mirror bonuses, keepsakes, House Contractor upgrades, Pact settings, and other later systems; first-run god offerings are also restricted compared with normal runs. Treat exact implementation details as useful but not as the design essence. The essence is that the first run is a clean “base-body” run before the meta systems open.

---

## What Hades needs to teach

Hades has to onboard a lot more than “move and attack.”

It must teach:

- real-time combat: attack, special, dash, cast, positioning, invulnerability timing, target priority;
- room structure: chamber locks, enemy waves, clear condition, reward, exit;
- temporary growth: boons, poms, hearts, money, shops, in-run build identity;
- permanent growth: Darkness, Chthonic Keys, Mirror of Night, weapon unlocks, House Contractor, relationships;
- narrative loop: Olympians talk during runs, House characters talk after runs, bosses remember you;
- roguelite psychology: death is expected, repetition is meaningful, failed attempts are story delivery;
- strategic routing: door reward symbols, blue/gold laurel distinction, risk/reward rooms;
- emotional stakes: escape is family rebellion, not abstract dungeon climbing.

The first attempt does not teach every system. It teaches the player how to think about systems that will keep unfolding.

# Moment-by-moment breakdown

## 1. Title and new file: the game frames itself as myth, not “mode select”

The title screen and presentation immediately signal that this is not a sterile roguelike ruleset. It is mythic, voiced, painterly, musical, and character-driven.

This matters because Hades is going to ask the player to repeat content many times. Repetition can feel mechanical. The game pre-emptively solves this by making repetition feel like retelling.

A normal roguelite might begin with: choose class, choose dungeon, start run.

Hades begins with mythic framing and a protagonist already in conflict.

The onboarding question is not “what is the optimal build?” yet. The first question is “why is this prince trying to leave?”

That question gives the player permission to care before they understand.

---

## 2. Opening narration: the player starts inside an act of defiance

The game does not begin with Zagreus waking up peacefully in the House and receiving a quest. It begins with him already attempting escape.

That is a major design choice.

If the player started in the House, the first minutes would be social, optional, menu-like, and spatially ambiguous. Instead, Hades starts in the dungeon, where the affordances are obvious:

- move;
- hit enemies;
- survive;
- clear rooms;
- go forward.

The player learns the action layer first. The House layer is deliberately withheld until death.

That sequencing is the whole trick.

The player first thinks:

> This is an action dungeon crawler.

After death, the player realizes:

> This is an action dungeon crawler wrapped inside a homecoming loop, social hub, and meta-progression engine.

The delayed reveal makes the hub feel earned rather than administrative.

---

## 3. First playable frame: no hub, no loadout, no planning burden

Zagreus begins in Tartarus with the Stygian Blade. There is no build screen, no weapon choice, no keepsake choice, no Mirror setup, no Pact of Punishment, no House Contractor, no codex to manage, no companion, no heat, no aspect selection.

This is not merely because those systems are locked. It is because the first run is a controlled base-state experiment.

The player has one body:

- 50 base health;
- one dash;
- the sword;
- no permanent upgrades;
- no death safety net;
- no weapon identity decision.

That matters because Hades will later become a game about layered preparation. But preparation is meaningless before the player understands what they are preparing for.

The first run removes preparation so the player can feel the base game.

This is analogous to Mega Man X starting without dash. Mega Man X delays dash so wall-jump and weakness can land. Hades delays the House systems so death and return can land.

---

## 4. First chamber boundary: every room is a tiny contract

Hades rooms are bounded arenas. Doors lock. Enemies spawn. Combat happens. When the room is cleared, a reward appears and exits unlock.

This is a critical roguelite onboarding shape because it gives the player a repeatable mental model:

1. enter chamber;
2. read enemy composition;
3. fight;
4. clear;
5. collect reward;
6. choose next chamber.

The chamber is not just a physical container. It is a learning container.

The player does not need to understand the whole Underworld. They need to understand the current chamber. That makes a complex procedural game feel approachable.

Hades’ first onboarding unit is not a tutorial prompt. It is the locked room.

---

## 5. Explicit controls: Hades uses prompts, but keeps them subordinate

Unlike Mega Man X, Hades uses explicit input prompts. This is not a weakness.

The game has more simultaneous verbs than classic action platformers:

- attack;
- special;
- dash;
- cast;
- interact;
- later call;
- later reload/weapon-specific actions;
- later companion;
- later keepsake and boon logic.

A no-text purity test would be bad design here. The important thing is that prompts are attached to immediate play, not abstract lecture.

The prompt does not replace the situation. It labels what the player is already trying to do.

Bad tutorial text says:

> Here are all the systems you will need later.

Hades says:

> You are in a room. Enemies are coming. These are your verbs.

The player learns by doing, but the game is not precious about withholding button names.

Design principle:

> Explicit instruction is good when it reduces control friction without stealing discovery.

---

## 6. Stygian Blade attack: the default verb is short-range commitment

The first weapon is the sword. That is important.

The Stygian Blade is direct, readable, and risky. To attack, Zagreus usually has to be near enemies. The blade’s combo gives immediate feedback: hit sparks, knockback, enemy flinch, and damage numbers.

The sword teaches three core combat facts:

- Hades is close-quarters first;
- attacking has positional commitment;
- survival depends on movement, not only damage.

A bow or shield would teach a very different first lesson. A ranged weapon might let the player kite before learning enemy timing. The sword forces the player to share space with danger.

That is valuable because Hades combat is fundamentally about rhythm:

> enter range, strike, dash out, re-enter, punish, reposition.

The first weapon encodes that rhythm.

---

## 7. Dash: the actual survival verb

Dash is not merely movement speed. It is the player’s panic button, spacing tool, dodge, approach, disengage, and later damage delivery mechanism through dash boons and dash-strikes.

In many action games, movement is separate from combat. In Hades, movement is combat.

The first rooms teach that standing still is death. Enemies telegraph, converge, throw projectiles, and occupy space. The correct response is rarely just “attack harder.” It is:

- dash through danger;
- dash behind an enemy;
- dash out of a projectile lane;
- dash into range;
- dash away after a slow special;
- dash toward a reward or exit after the room clears.

The player’s first important skill is not DPS. It is not getting hit.

This matters because the Mirror later offers Greater Reflex, which gives an extra dash. The player can only value that upgrade if the first run has already made dash feel like life.

---

## 8. Special and cast: secondary verbs are available before they are necessary

The sword’s special is slower and broader than basic attack. The cast gives a ranged projectile. Both are available immediately, but the first room does not require mastery of either.

This is good onboarding because optional verbs create expressive discovery.

The player can survive early rooms with basic attack and dash. But if they try special or cast, they feel:

- special: area coverage and delay;
- cast: range, projectile targeting, and later Bloodstone behavior;
- dash-strike: attack/movement fusion.

The game lets the player find extra power without demanding it too soon.

This is one of Hades’ recurring patterns:

> Make the minimum viable solution simple, but let advanced behavior be discoverable immediately.

---

## 9. Enemy spawn presentation: danger appears before it acts

Enemies in Tartarus tend to appear with readable spawn and attack timings. The player often sees where danger is forming before it becomes lethal.

This is crucial because Hades is visually rich. Without telegraph discipline, the screen would become noise.

The first enemies are not conceptually complex. They mainly teach:

- enemies can spawn in waves;
- rooms can add threats after the initial set;
- enemies telegraph before attacking;
- enemy death is immediate, clear, and satisfying;
- the chamber does not end until all enemies are gone.

This is the roguelite equivalent of the first Spikey in Mega Man X. Low conceptual load. High information value.

---

## 10. First room clear: the combat loop breathes

When the first room is cleared, combat stops. A reward appears. Doors unlock.

This pause is not dead time. It is the other half of the game.

Hades’ core pacing alternates:

- combat intensity;
- reward choice;
- navigation choice;
- dialogue/interruption;
- combat again.

The first clear teaches the player that Hades is not a continuous gauntlet. It is a sequence of discrete tests with reflection points between them.

That reflection rhythm is what makes later build decisions possible. The player needs safe moments to inspect boons, health, doors, coins, and upgrades.

---

## 11. First boon: a reward is also a person

The first god boon is one of the most important onboarding moments in the game.

A weaker game would say:

> Choose one of three modifiers.

Hades says:

> A god is speaking to you. They know who you are. They want to help. Choose their gift.

That changes the reward’s category.

A boon is simultaneously:

- a temporary mechanical upgrade;
- a build-defining choice;
- a character introduction;
- a piece of worldbuilding;
- a promise that the Olympians are watching.

This is why Hades can sustain so many repeated runs. Rewards are not just numbers. They are relationships.

Community documentation indicates the first boon is guaranteed to be Athena and typically includes attack, cast, and dash options. The design reason is obvious: Athena’s Deflect mechanic is defensive, legible, and empowering. If the player selects a Deflect boon, hostile projectiles can become reflected agency.

That is a brilliant first divine modifier because it reframes defense as active power.

---

## 12. The boon choice teaches build authorship immediately

The first boon does not merely say “you got stronger.” It asks, “what part of yourself do you want to change?”

Attack boon:

> My default damage verb is altered.

Dash boon:

> My survival/movement verb is altered.

Cast boon:

> My ranged/secondary verb is altered.

This is a small but important decision. The player does not understand builds yet, but they understand preference.

Do I attack a lot? Pick attack.

Do I dash constantly? Pick dash.

Do I like ranged shots? Pick cast.

The game does not ask for optimization knowledge. It asks for self-observation.

That is great onboarding.

---

## 13. Immediate post-boon chamber: the game pays off the choice fast

After receiving a boon, the next combat space lets the player feel the modification.

This is essential. A reward with delayed feedback is hard to learn. Hades makes boons sensory:

- new visual effects;
- new sound effects;
- different damage behavior;
- deflected projectiles;
- status effects;
- impact changes.

The player starts to build a causal model:

> I chose this. Now my verb behaves differently.

That model is the foundation of the whole roguelite.

---

## 14. Door symbols: the game begins teaching strategic literacy

After chambers, exits show reward symbols. Early on, the player may not know what every symbol means. That is fine.

The first job of the door symbols is not full comprehension. It is recognition.

The player learns:

- doors preview rewards;
- the next chamber is chosen by reward desire;
- different icons represent different types of growth;
- route choice is part of play, not a menu outside play.

Later, the player learns blue laurels versus gold laurels: persistent resources versus run-specific rewards. That distinction is one of Hades’ most important routing concepts.

But the first few doors can be understood more simply:

> This symbol means I will get that thing if I survive the next room.

That is enough.

---

## 15. Darkness: mysterious currency becomes death-proof hope

Darkness is one of the smartest early rewards because its purpose is not fully meaningful until the player returns to the House.

In-run, Darkness feels like a strange collectible. It is not immediately as legible as health or a boon. The player may think it is score, currency, XP, or lore.

After death, the Mirror reveals the truth:

> Darkness is permanent self-improvement.

That reveal makes the previous run retroactively meaningful.

The player thought they failed. Then they realize they brought something home.

This is the core Hades onboarding move:

> Let the player collect meaning before explaining the meaning, then reveal that failure preserved it.

---

## 16. Chthonic Keys: locked future as reward

Keys are another early metagame seed. A key is not immediately power in the hand. It is a promise:

- new weapons;
- more Mirror talents;
- broader strategic identity.

Keys teach that some rewards expand the possibility space rather than improving the current run.

This matters because Hades has two different forms of progression:

- vertical progression: more health, more damage, more survivability;
- horizontal progression: new weapons, new talents, new choices, new builds.

Keys are horizontal progression. The player does not need to understand that immediately. The important thing is that the key icon feels important and different.

---

## 17. Obols and Charon: temporary money has a face

When the player collects gold/Obols, they learn a run-specific economy. Charon’s shop teaches where that money goes.

The shop is a safe room. No enemies. No rush. Just prices and offerings.

That contrast matters. The shop is not just an economy tutorial. It is a breath.

Charon himself is silent, strange, and memorable. Again, Hades personifies a system:

- shopkeeper;
- ferry myth;
- run economy;
- later combat secret;
- later relationship.

A normal roguelite shop says “buy item.”

Hades says “this silent boatman is part of your escape ecosystem.”

---

## 18. Centaur Hearts: the player learns temporary survivability

A Centaur Heart increases life total for the current run.

That is a different kind of reward from Darkness.

Darkness says:

> This run matters even if I die.

A Heart says:

> This run itself can go farther.

The first time the player sees max health rise, they learn that each attempt has its own internal growth arc. The run begins small and can become stronger before it ends.

This is important because Hades is not merely about permanent grinding. A run itself has a life story.

---

## 19. Poms: upgrades can modify upgrades

A Pom of Power upgrades an existing boon. The first Pom teaches that boons are not static selections. They can grow.

This adds a second-order build concept:

- choose a boon;
- then choose whether to invest further in it.

That is a sophisticated idea, but Hades makes it intuitive. The Pom presents existing boons and increases numbers. The player sees their earlier choice become more important.

Good onboarding does not teach “buildcrafting” as a spreadsheet. It teaches attachment.

> I picked this attack boon. Now I can make my attack boon better.

---

## 20. Traps: the room itself is a combat participant

Tartarus includes hazards such as spike traps. These are readable, delayed, and can often hurt enemies too.

This is important because Hades combat is not just player versus enemies. It is player, enemies, hazards, walls, pillars, projectiles, and reward incentives inside a bounded arena.

The first traps teach:

- the floor matters;
- standing still is dangerous;
- enemies can be lured;
- the environment is not purely decorative;
- damage sources can be neutral or opportunistic.

This keeps combat from becoming “spam attack until room clear.”

---

## 21. Projectile enemies: dash becomes proof, not theory

The first projectile-heavy enemies teach why dash matters.

Melee enemies teach spacing. Projectile enemies teach timing and lanes.

If the player has Athena Deflect, projectiles become even more educational because the player can see the difference between dodging and redirecting. If they chose a different boon, they still learn the importance of dashing across danger lines.

This is where the first boon and enemy design can combine.

Good onboarding does not merely introduce A and B. It introduces A, introduces B, then shows how A changes B.

---

## 22. Armor and tougher enemies: not every hit has the same meaning

As Tartarus progresses, enemies become more durable or dangerous. Some have armor or elite properties later in the run structure.

The player learns that “enemy” is not one category. Enemies differ by:

- speed;
- attack delay;
- range;
- projectile pattern;
- health;
- armor;
- summon behavior;
- arena interaction.

The early game introduces these differences gradually. Hades does not need the player to memorize names. It needs the player to read behavior.

The onboarding target is not taxonomy. It is pattern literacy.

---

## 23. Branching choices: strategy begins when reward options conflict

The first time the player sees two exits, the game changes from linear survival to strategic desire.

The player may choose between:

- a god boon;
- a key;
- Darkness;
- a Heart;
- gold;
- a shop;
- a Pom;
- later hammers, chaos, trials, NPCs, etc.

The first branch does not require expert knowledge. It asks a simple question:

> What do I want more right now?

That question is the seed of Hades strategy.

Over time, it becomes:

- Do I need survival or damage?
- Do I want permanent resources or a stronger current run?
- Is my build missing a core boon?
- Can I afford Charon?
- Is a skull-room reward worth the risk?

But first, it is just desire.

---

## 24. Skull-marked rewards and mini-bosses: risk is previewed

Some room rewards show a small skull marker, indicating a tougher encounter.

This is excellent because the game does not ambush the player with risk without signaling it. It previews difficulty through the same door language as reward.

That teaches a crucial roguelite principle:

> The player is responsible for choosing some of their own risk.

The game is not only testing execution. It is testing judgment.

---

## 25. Low health: the first run teaches fragility on purpose

On the first run, the player lacks Mirror upgrades such as Death Defiance, extra dash, or extra health. This makes the first attempt fragile.

That fragility is useful.

If the player survived too long too easily, death would arrive late and feel like punishment. If the player dies at a reasonable pace, death becomes the next tutorial beat.

Hades wants the player to die not because it is cruel, but because death unlocks the actual structure.

The first run is therefore tuned as a controlled failure state.

This is similar to Mega Man X’s Vile encounter, except Hades allows the failure point to vary. Vile is fixed. Hades’ death reveal is systemic.

---

## 26. Death screen: “There is no escape” is thesis, not scolding

When Zagreus dies, the game does not say “Game Over.”

The famous idea is: there is no escape.

That statement has multiple meanings:

- You failed this attempt.
- The Underworld is structurally designed to keep you here.
- Death does not free you from the loop.
- The story and mechanics are the same thing.

A normal Game Over ejects the player from fiction. Hades’ death screen deepens the fiction.

The player does not stop being Zagreus when they lose. Losing is one of Zagreus’ canonical experiences.

That is the gold-star move.

---

## 27. Pool of Styx: death becomes arrival

Zagreus emerges from the Pool of Styx in the House of Hades.

This is the single most important onboarding moment in the game.

The player expected reset. Instead, they get place.

The House is not a menu. It is a spatialized consequence of death.

This accomplishes several things at once:

- it normalizes dying;
- it preserves narrative continuity;
- it introduces the hub only when it is meaningful;
- it gives the player somewhere safe to process failure;
- it turns death into social re-entry.

The player has not “gone back to start.” They have returned home.

But home is also the prison they are trying to escape.

That contradiction is the emotional engine of the game.

---

## 28. Hypnos: failure becomes personalized feedback

Hypnos often comments on how Zagreus died.

This is incredibly smart.

A death recap screen could feel clinical:

> Killed by Wretched Thug. Damage taken: X. Time survived: Y.

Hypnos turns that into comedy, personality, and frictionless feedback.

The death source becomes a social event. The game remembers. Someone noticed.

This makes losing feel less lonely.

It also gives the game a way to acknowledge failure without shaming the player. Hypnos is often goofy, detached, or accidentally insulting, which softens the emotional sting.

Design principle:

> Feedback lands better when it comes from a character whose tone fits the world.

---

## 29. Hades at the desk: antagonist as administrator

Hades is not introduced only as a final boss. He is your father, your landlord, your boss, and the administrator of the dead.

When the player returns, Hades is there, working. That is a brilliant inversion.

In many games, the villain waits at the end. In Hades, the villain is at home.

This does several things:

- it makes the conflict intimate;
- it keeps the antagonist present between runs;
- it makes escape feel like family rebellion;
- it gives the father/son dynamic constant reinforcement;
- it makes the House feel politically and emotionally charged.

Hades’ dismissiveness reframes the failed run. You are not merely bad at the game. You are a son being belittled by a powerful father.

That is emotionally useful because it gives the player a reason to try again beyond “beat the game.”

The player wants to prove him wrong.

---

## 30. Cerberus: safe affection inside the hostile House

Cerberus is one of the most important early House elements because he gives the player a low-stakes, emotionally warm interaction.

Petting or speaking to Cerberus teaches that the House is not only a failure lobby. It is a relationship space.

This matters because Hades is full of hostile framing: punishment, death, fatherly contempt, impossible escape. Cerberus creates counterweight.

The House becomes complex:

- oppressive because Hades controls it;
- bureaucratic because souls are processed there;
- safe because no combat happens there;
- warm because some characters care about Zagreus;
- painful because it is home but not freedom.

This emotional complexity makes repeated returns bearable.

---

## 31. Nyx and Achilles: mentorship appears after failure

Nyx and Achilles provide early emotional scaffolding.

Their presence says:

> You are not alone in this rebellion.

This is another way Hades softens roguelite failure. The player dies, then returns not to silence, but to people with perspective, concern, advice, history, and secrets.

The House conversations also teach a vital rule:

> Dialogue advances because you attempted escape.

That means even a bad run can produce new story.

This is one of Hades’ most powerful anti-frustration systems. The player may think:

> I died early, but maybe someone will have something new to say.

That is enough to make another run appealing.

---

## 32. The Mirror of Night: permanent progression becomes physically located

The Mirror of Night is in Zagreus’ room. It lets the player spend Darkness to improve Zagreus across attempts.

This is the key recontextualization of the first run.

Before the Mirror:

> Darkness was a weird purple reward.

After the Mirror:

> Darkness is future survival.

The Mirror is not hidden in an abstract upgrade menu. It is in the bedroom. That matters.

Permanent progression is not a spreadsheet outside the story. It is a private act of preparation, aided by Nyx, inside the House.

This is how Hades makes meta-progression feel diegetic.

The first Mirror talents are also well chosen because they map onto pain the player has already felt:

- more damage from behind;
- small healing between chambers;
- later, extra lives;
- later, extra dash;
- later, more max health.

The player is not optimizing in the abstract. They are responding to remembered pain.

Good meta-progression lets the player say:

> I know why I want this.

---

## 33. Zagreus’ bedroom: the private reset space

The bedroom is not merely where the Mirror lives. It is the place where the game turns combat failure into personal preparation.

Spatially, the player moves from:

- public judgment in the Great Hall;
- father and shades;
- Hypnos commentary;
- mentors and household;
- private room;
- Mirror;
- then out to the courtyard.

That sequence is emotionally coherent.

The player is not dumped into an upgrade menu immediately after death. They walk through the social consequences of death first.

Then they choose how to improve.

That order matters.

---

## 34. Courtyard and Skelly: safe practice after real danger

After the House, the player reaches the training courtyard. Skelly appears as a safe target.

This is an excellent inversion of the first chamber.

The first run taught combat under pressure. The courtyard lets the player test moves without pressure.

That is the right order for Hades.

If the game opened with Skelly as a static tutorial dummy, it would feel like training before context. Instead, after dying, Skelly becomes useful:

> I now know combat is dangerous. I want to test my weapon safely.

The player’s need appears before the training object.

That is exactly the same principle as Mega Man X’s wall-jump pit: create need, then make the mechanic obvious.

---

## 35. Weapon rack: future identity is locked but visible

The Infernal Arms are displayed in the courtyard. Some are unavailable until the player has enough Chthonic Keys.

This is good aspirational onboarding.

The game shows:

- there are more playstyles;
- keys matter;
- future runs can feel different;
- the current weapon is not the whole game.

Locked weapons are not just restrictions. They are visible promises.

The player leaves the first death loop understanding that repetition will not only mean “same sword again forever.” The game will widen.

---

## 36. Starting the second run: repetition is now transformed

The second escape attempt is where Hades proves its onboarding thesis.

The player is going back to Tartarus, but it is not the same experience.

Something has changed in at least one of these dimensions:

- the player understands enemy patterns better;
- the player may have spent Darkness;
- the player may have unlocked or approached a weapon unlock;
- characters have new dialogue;
- the player understands that death is productive;
- the player has seen the House;
- the player has emotional reasons to continue.

This is the core roguelite problem solved:

> How do you make repetition feel like progress?

Hades answers:

> By making repetition mechanically, narratively, socially, and emotionally non-identical.

---

# First-time Tartarus boss: Megaera as the first “level boss” equivalent

A first-time player may not reach Megaera on the first attempt, but Megaera is the end-of-Tartarus boss and the closest Hades equivalent to “end of first level.”

## 37. Pre-boss ramp: the player has already learned the room grammar

By the time the player reaches Megaera, they have cleared many chambers. They understand:

- rooms lock;
- enemies spawn in waves;
- rewards follow combat;
- doors preview future rewards;
- health attrition matters;
- boons change verbs;
- dash is survival;
- traps matter;
- some fights are harder than others.

Megaera is not teaching the basic chamber loop. She is testing whether the player can apply it under boss pressure.

This is good sequencing.

A boss should not be the first time the player learns how the game works. A boss should be where the player discovers whether their mental model holds.

---

## 38. Megaera’s identity: the boss is not anonymous

Megaera is not just “Tartarus Boss 1.” She is a Fury, a character, and later a recurring House presence.

The first time she confronts Zagreus, the player feels a sharper version of the Hades pattern:

> Combat opponents are also people in this household/mythic ecosystem.

This is essential to the game’s onboarding because bosses remembering you is one of Hades’ signature promises.

The boss is not disposable content. She is part of the social loop.

---

## 39. Megaera’s arena: simple enough to focus on pattern reading

Megaera’s arena is relatively readable. It gives room to move but not infinite safety.

The arena’s job is to center the boss pattern rather than the environment.

This is important because the player’s first boss should test:

- dash timing;
- telegraph recognition;
- patience;
- target focus;
- recovery under pressure;
- wave management when adds appear.

The first boss should not overcomplicate navigation at the same time.

---

## 40. Megaera’s attacks: each pattern tests a different early skill

Megaera’s kit is a curriculum.

Her lunges test lateral dash timing.

Her close-range whip pressure tests whether the player can stop face-tanking and disengage.

Her projectile patterns test lane reading and repeated evasion.

Her area-of-effect markings test floor awareness.

Her summoned enemies test target prioritization under boss pressure.

The boss therefore asks:

> Did you learn to move, read, dodge, attack, and manage space, or were you only clearing rooms by brute force?

This is a proper first-boss exam.

---

## 41. Losing to Megaera: defeat becomes relationship escalation

If the player dies to Megaera, they return to the House. Hypnos may comment. Hades may needle them. Later interactions with Megaera can change.

This is vital.

In most action games, losing to a boss means replaying until victory. In Hades, losing to a boss means:

- you learned her pattern;
- you may have gained resources;
- you may get new dialogue;
- you may upgrade;
- you may return stronger;
- the boss becomes part of your social world.

That turns boss failure into a memory, not a wall.

---

## 42. Beating Megaera: first biome victory teaches bounties and expansion

The first victory over Megaera awards Titan Blood. This is a major long-term resource tied to weapons and future progression.

This is smart because the first biome clear does not merely say “next area unlocked.” It says:

> Bosses are resource gates.

The player learns that major victories produce different rewards from ordinary rooms.

The game also opens the next biome, Asphodel, which changes environment, enemy behavior, traversal hazards, and run ambition.

A first boss victory therefore expands both horizontal and vertical understanding:

- new region;
- new resource class;
- stronger confidence;
- new story reactions;
- future weapon progression.

# The key design moves

## Move 1: Start with action, delay the hub

Hades withholds the House until after death. This is the central onboarding decision.

If the House came first, it would be a confusing menu-space full of characters, rooms, and systems the player could not value yet.

By placing the first run before the hub, the game makes the player need the hub.

After dying, the player wants:

- explanation;
- safety;
- upgrades;
- emotional response;
- a way to try again.

Then the House provides all of those.

Create need first. Reveal system second.

---

## Move 2: Make failure the gateway to more content

In many games, failure closes possibility. In Hades, failure opens it.

Death unlocks:

- new dialogue;
- Mirror spending;
- House movement;
- weapon courtyard use;
- character relationships;
- more context;
- a new run with changed understanding.

The player is not asked to tolerate death because “that is the genre.” The player is rewarded for dying with new texture.

This is why Hades is S-tier for roguelite onboarding.

---

## Move 3: Personify systems

Hades avoids sterile abstraction by turning systems into characters.

- Boons are Olympian relationships.
- Death recap is Hypnos.
- Shop is Charon.
- Meta-progression is Nyx’s Mirror.
- Boss wall is Megaera.
- Final authority is Hades.
- Emotional safety is Cerberus.
- Practice dummy is Skelly.

This is one of the strongest transferable lessons.

If a system repeats often, give it personality.

Personality makes repetition tolerable. Relationship makes repetition desirable.

---

## Move 4: Split rewards into temporary and permanent, then let players feel both

Hades teaches two reward economies:

Temporary run power:

- boons;
- hearts;
- poms;
- obols;
- shops;
- hammers later.

Permanent or meta progression:

- Darkness;
- keys;
- gemstones;
- nectar;
- Titan Blood;
- keepsakes;
- weapons;
- contractor upgrades;
- relationships.

The first loop teaches both categories emotionally.

Temporary power helps you survive now.

Permanent power makes death less final.

That dual economy is why a failed run can still feel productive.

---

## Move 5: Make every run narratively non-identical

The genius of Hades is not only that runs are mechanically randomized. They are narratively refreshed.

Characters comment. Gods introduce themselves. Bosses remember. The House changes. Relationship lines unlock. Zagreus reacts.

This prevents the player from evaluating a failed run only by combat distance.

Even if they died early, they may still think:

> I wonder who has something new to say.

That is a powerful retention loop because curiosity survives mechanical failure.

---

## Move 6: Teach systems at the moment of emotional relevance

Hades does not frontload every explanation.

- Darkness is explained when you return and can spend it.
- Weapon unlocks matter once you have keys and have felt the sword.
- Keepsakes matter once relationships and gifts matter.
- The contractor matters once the House is familiar.
- Heat/Pact matters once escape has been achieved.
- Advanced boon synergies matter once basic boons are understood.

This is exemplary sequencing.

Do not explain a system just because it exists.

Explain it when the player has a reason to care.

# Direct comparison to Mega Man X

Mega Man X and Hades solve different onboarding problems with the same deeper philosophy.

Mega Man X’s problem:

> How do you teach a faster, more expressive sequel without slowing down a platformer?

Mega Man X’s solution:

> Put the player in a designed sequence where each mechanic becomes necessary, then make weakness the emotional motivation for progression.

Hades’ problem:

> How do you teach a genre where repeated death is expected without making death feel like failure or wasted time?

Hades’ solution:

> Make death diegetic, social, narratively productive, and mechanically useful.

Mega Man X’s first level teaches:

> You are not strong enough yet.

Hades’ first loop teaches:

> You are not done when you die.

Both games align mechanic and theme.

In Mega Man X, becoming stronger is story, upgrades, and player desire.

In Hades, trying again is story, progression, and player desire.

# Transferable principles for designing Hades-like onboarding

## 1. The first failure should reveal a system, not just reset the player

If your game expects failure, failure must have content.

After a first death, give at least one of:

- new dialogue;
- new upgrade access;
- new route access;
- new character reaction;
- new player knowledge;
- new equipment possibility;
- new story layer.

Failure should say:

> Now you understand more of the game.

Not:

> Do the same thing again.

## 2. Delay complex preparation until the player has felt the pain it solves

Do not ask the player to choose a build before they understand damage, survival, spacing, or failure.

First: let them feel weak.

Then: offer upgrades that answer the weakness.

## 3. Personify repeated systems

Any system the player sees dozens of times should have flavor, voice, or relational context.

A shop can be a character.

A death recap can be a character.

An upgrade menu can be a room.

A difficulty gate can be a rival.

A reward can be a god.

## 4. Make temporary and permanent rewards visibly different

Players need to understand what helps the current attempt versus what survives the attempt.

Do not blur these categories too early.

A great roguelite onboarding loop gives players both:

- something that helps now;
- something that makes even death worthwhile.

## 5. Every repeated attempt should have at least one new axis

A second run should not be merely “run one again.”

It can differ through:

- player knowledge;
- small upgrade;
- new dialogue;
- new reward type;
- new weapon option;
- changed enemy combination;
- changed route decision;
- changed emotional context.

The player must feel that repetition is accumulating meaning.

## 6. The hub should answer an emotional need, not just organize features

Hades’ House works because the player arrives after dying.

The player wants safety, explanation, reaction, and a way back out.

The House provides those in one place.

If your game has a hub, ask:

> What emotional state does the player enter the hub with?

Then design the hub to meet that state.

---

# Why Hades belongs in the S-tier onboarding library

Hades is the gold-star reference for death-loop onboarding.

It teaches the player that:

- death is expected;
- death is narrative;
- death is social;
- death is mechanically productive;
- repeated runs are not repeated story states;
- temporary rewards make the current attempt exciting;
- permanent rewards make failed attempts meaningful;
- characters can carry system explanation without feeling like tutorial boxes.

The first run is not meant to be won.

It is meant to transform the player’s definition of losing.

That is the design achievement.
