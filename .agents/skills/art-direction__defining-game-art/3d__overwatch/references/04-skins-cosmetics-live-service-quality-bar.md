## 1. Cosmetic quality definition

An Overwatch-class skin is not a costume prompt. It is a **recognizable gameplay identity remixed into a new fantasy**.

A passing skin must:

- preserve hero recognition
- preserve weapon/tool read
- preserve movement and rig logic
- preserve enemy-facing VFX recognition
- preserve hit-read and perceived mass
- express a clear theme
- feel manufactured/equipped inside the game’s world
- deliver premium novelty without damaging fairness
- create delight at close range while staying readable at combat range

The best skins feel like:

```text
same hero, new fantasy, no gameplay relearning
```

---

## 2. Skin parity contract

Every skin must pass this contract.

| Dimension | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Silhouette | Core hero outline remains recognizable in idle, run, ability, death, and partial cover. |
| Posture | Stance and carriage remain hero-specific. |
| Weapon/tool | Gameplay prop remains identifiable by shape, length, aim line, and firing source. |
| Animation | Costume supports existing rig/animation without implying different mechanics. |
| VFX | Enemy-facing ability shape/timing/function remains stable. |
| Hit-read | Perceived bulk does not imply false hitbox or hide true hitbox. |
| Team read | Skin does not collide with ally/enemy/state colors in harmful ways. |
| Performance | Added material/VFX complexity does not compromise platform stability or visibility. |
| Fantasy | New theme is strong enough to justify the skin. |

A skin that is beautiful but breaks parity is not S-tier. It is production debt.

## 3. Identity anchors and silhouette limits

### Core identity anchors

Define each hero’s non-negotiable anchors before skin ideation:

```text
head/helmet/hair anchor
shoulder/torso mass
weapon/tool shape
backpack/wing/cloak/mech/appendage anchor
stance/base width
locomotion signature
primary VFX shape
```

### Change bands

Classify each zone:

| Zone | Meaning | Skin freedom |
|---|---|---|
| Locked | Critical to recognition or gameplay. | Do not meaningfully alter. |
| Constrained | Can change but must maintain mass/line/function. | Moderate remix. |
| Free | Decorative/theme zone. | High remix. |
| Close-up reward | Details visible in hero select/inspect. | High detail if not noisy. |

### Silhouette-safe change strategies

- Change material, not mass.
- Add motifs inside the silhouette rather than outside it.
- Use color/value shifts to differentiate from prior skins.
- Preserve weapon length and aim line.
- Turn large external additions into flat/close-to-body forms.
- Put theme into surface finish, decals, mechanical details, and focal accents.
- Make big additions correspond to existing anchors: shoulder, backpack, coat hem, headgear, weapon housing.

### Silhouette-risk additions

Treat these as high risk:

- wings
- capes/cloaks
- tall hats/horns
- giant shoulder pads
- floating companions
- long trailing ribbons
- animal heads/tails
- huge backpacks
- oversized weapons
- skirt/coat shapes that alter leg read
- glowing outlines that mask true silhouette

High risk does not mean forbidden. It means the skin needs extra silhouette, animation, and gameplay tests.

## 4. Theme translation

A weak skin pastes a theme. A strong skin translates a theme through the hero.

### Translation ladder

```text
theme reference → hero fit → silhouette-safe motif → material logic → weapon/tool integration → VFX/audio flavor → close-up details
```

### Theme-fit questions

Ask:

```text
Why this hero for this theme?
What part of the hero fantasy does the theme amplify?
What part of the theme must be visible at distance?
What part should only be close-up reward?
How does the weapon/tool become part of the fantasy?
How do materials make the theme feel built, not pasted?
What must not change because of gameplay recognition?
```

### Good theme fit

A strong theme creates a natural mapping:

- healer → botanical, angelic, medical, ritual, restoration, water, light
- brawler → storm, rock, metal, beast, scrap, thunder, sports fighter
- sniper → noble hunter, assassin, serpent, hawk, telescope, military precision
- mobility hero → wind, fox, motorcycle, dancer, comet, courier
- shield/tank → fortress, knight, mountain, titan, turtle, mech, guardian

### Bad theme fit

Failure often looks like:

- all heroes get the same costume silhouette
- theme ignores weapon/tool
- motif contradicts hero personality
- cultural symbols are used as decals only
- premium detail is randomly distributed
- the hero becomes unrecognizable outside hero select

## 5. Material, construction, and world believability

Overwatch-class cosmetics often combine recognizable real-world/thematic motifs with futuristic construction logic.

### Manufactured, not stitched-on

Even goofy or fantastical skins should feel like they could function:

- how does armor attach?
- where does the weapon fire from?
- what powers the tech?
- how does the character move in this outfit?
- what materials are soft, hard, flexible, reactive, glowing?
- what parts are decorative vs functional?
- how does the skin fit the hero’s world/faction/class?

### Material hierarchy

Use material to clarify, not clutter:

```text
primary material mass: carries theme
secondary material: supports construction
accent material: focal/premium detail
emissive/VFX: state, power, or small fantasy flourish
```

Avoid using all materials everywhere.

### Close-up reward

Close-up details are valuable when they do not hurt combat read:

- small logos
- thematic charms
- fabric patterns
- engraved motifs
- mechanical labels
- cultural references
- wear marks
- personal artifacts

Close-up details should be discoverable, not required.

## 6. Cultural and mythology-inspired skins

Culturally inspired skins require more responsibility than generic fantasy skins.

### Research standard

A high-quality cultural skin should use:

- multiple primary or high-quality reference sources
- historical/cultural context
- material and garment research
- motif meanings
- regional specificity
- avoidance of generic pan-cultural collage
- consultation when stakes are high

### Integration standard

Do not paste symbols. Integrate them through:

- silhouette-safe garment structure
- material and surface finish
- weapon/tool adaptation
- color palette with intentional meaning
- culturally specific pattern placement
- respectful motif use
- character fit

### Character-fit standard

The theme should amplify something true about the hero:

```text
personality
role
weapon/tool
origin/story
movement style
existing silhouette
relationship to myth/archetype
```

### Avoid

- costume-party feel
- sacred symbols as random decals
- mixing unrelated cultures because it “looks cool”
- reducing culture to color/pattern
- compromising recognition for authenticity theater
- ignoring the weapon/tool

## 7. Rarity and premium value

Rarity should increase value without increasing gameplay confusion.

### Suggested rarity ladder

| Tier | Added value | Risk controls |
|---|---|---|
| Recolor / basic | Palette shift, minor material changes. | Preserve contrast and team readability. |
| Epic-style | Stronger material/pattern theme, modest model changes. | Keep silhouette very close. |
| Legendary-style | New theme, material, model treatment, weapon redesign within constraints. | Full parity tests. |
| Mythic/customizable | Multiple selectable variants, richer VFX/audio/UI flourish. | Enemy-facing consistency and platform budgets. |
| Crossover/premium event | External IP/theme translation. | Dual identity balance and strict gameplay parity. |

### Premium value should come from

- theme intelligence
- material quality
- weapon/tool integration
- close-up detail
- hero-specific personality
- tasteful VFX/audio variants
- animation/pose/emote extras where safe
- customization options that do not change enemy read

Premium value should not come from:

- bigger silhouette
- harder-to-see materials
- misleading projectiles
- louder enemy confusion
- false hitbox reads
- visual clutter in combat

## 8. Mythic/customizable skins

Mythic skins are high-risk because they invite more change.

### Mythic rule

```text
Mythic customization can change fantasy, palette, armor pieces, weapon finish, and self/ally flourish; it must not change enemy-facing comprehension.
```

### Customization slots

Safer slots:

- palette/material variants
- small armor plate swaps inside silhouette
- weapon surface finish
- inspect/hero-select effects
- self-view subtle elements
- post-impact flavor
- voice/intro/highlight presentation

Higher-risk slots:

- head/helmet silhouette
- shoulder mass
- weapon geometry
- cloak/cape/wings
- projectile shape
- ultimate boundary
- enemy-visible glow
- audio that changes detectability

### Mythic approval questions

```text
Can enemies still identify hero and ability instantly?
Does any variant look more/less visible on common maps?
Does any variant fake bulk or hitbox?
Does any variant hide aim, projectile, or ultimate boundary?
Does any variant create louder or quieter gameplay cues?
Are variants equally fair across platforms and settings?
```

## 9. Crossovers

Crossover skins have two identities to respect:

```text
the base hero + the crossover fantasy
```

The base hero must win in gameplay.

### Crossover translation rules

- Identify the crossover character’s 2–3 most iconic features.
- Map those features to existing hero anchors.
- Preserve hero silhouette/motion/weapon/hit-read.
- Avoid turning the hero into a different character with the wrong role read.
- Use material, color, decals, hairstyle/headgear, and weapon finish for IP recognition.
- Keep enemy-facing VFX stable.

### Crossover failure modes

- base hero unrecognizable
- crossover character unrecognizable
- both identities diluted
- weapon/tool does not fit either fantasy
- silhouette implies another hero
- skin becomes a mascot costume

## 10. Cosmetic VFX and audio

Cosmetic VFX/audio should enrich fantasy without altering gameplay comprehension.

### Enemy-facing constraints

Preserve:

- projectile shape family
- timing
- source location
- active boundary
- impact read
- ownership read
- dangerous duration
- auditory detectability level

### Safer enrichment zones

- self weapon charge effect
- hero select aura
- ally-side heal shimmer
- impact sparkle after the hit has been understood
- ultimate intro flourish before/after the gameplay-critical window
- inspect/emote/highlight effects

### Audio caution

Audio cosmetics can create pay-to-win/pay-to-lose behavior. Be careful with:

- footsteps
- projectile sounds
- reload cues
- ultimate activation
- ability charge sounds
- ambient skin sounds

A skin should not make a hero easier or harder to locate unless that is explicitly and fairly standardized.

## 11. Production gates

### Gate 1 — Skin concept fit

```text
[ ] Theme fits hero role/personality/tool.
[ ] Design is more than costume collage.
[ ] Core identity anchors are identified.
[ ] Risk zones are marked.
```

### Gate 2 — Silhouette parity

```text
[ ] Base and skin silhouettes are recognizable side by side.
[ ] Partial cover still reads.
[ ] Motion poses still read.
[ ] Major ability poses still read.
```

### Gate 3 — Weapon/tool parity

```text
[ ] Weapon length/shape/function still reads.
[ ] First-person view is aim-safe.
[ ] Third-person enemy source reads.
[ ] Reload/charge state remains clear.
```

### Gate 4 — VFX/audio parity

```text
[ ] Enemy-facing effects preserve shape/timing/function.
[ ] Ally/self effects do not create clutter.
[ ] Audio does not create unfair detectability changes.
```

### Gate 5 — Map visibility parity

Test against:

```text
bright map
dark map
warm interior
cool exterior
busy prop area
high VFX fight
low graphics settings if relevant
colorblind/contrast checks when applicable
```

### Gate 6 — Premium value

```text
[ ] Skin feels worth owning.
[ ] Close-up rewards exist.
[ ] Theme specificity is high.
[ ] Premium value does not depend on gameplay confusion.
```

## 12. Failure modes

### 12.1 Costume, not skin

Symptoms:

- theme pasted onto body
- no weapon/tool integration
- construction logic absent
- feels like Halloween costume

Fix:

- rebuild around material/construction logic
- integrate weapon/tool
- use hero-specific motifs
- remove generic costume parts

### 12.2 Silhouette break

Symptoms:

- giant wings/cape/horns/hat change combat outline
- leg/arm/weapon read altered
- mistaken for another hero

Fix:

- move motif inside silhouette
- reduce external mass
- map additions to existing anchors
- preserve weapon line and stance

### 12.3 Theme too weak

Symptoms:

- mostly recolor
- no unique material story
- close-up details generic
- theme could belong to any hero

Fix:

- choose sharper motif
- connect theme to hero verb
- add weapon/tool integration
- use distinctive material/color combination

### 12.4 Cultural collage

Symptoms:

- mixed symbols without context
- motif decals everywhere
- generic “Eastern,” “tribal,” “ancient,” “mythic” shorthand

Fix:

- narrow reference region/source
- identify motif meanings
- consult when needed
- integrate through garment/material/weapon logic

### 12.5 Premium unfairness

Symptoms:

- variant harder to see
- projectile changed
- enemy cues quieter/louder
- perceived hitbox differs
- ultimate boundary obscured

Fix:

- restore enemy-facing parity
- move richness to self/ally/hero-gallery view
- normalize audio and effect silhouette

---

## 13. Final checklist

```text
[ ] Same hero, new fantasy.
[ ] Core silhouette anchors preserved.
[ ] Posture and motion identity preserved.
[ ] Weapon/tool read preserved.
[ ] Rig/animation logic preserved.
[ ] Enemy-facing VFX/audio parity preserved.
[ ] Hit-read and perceived mass are fair.
[ ] Theme fits the hero specifically.
[ ] Material/construction feels manufactured, not pasted.
[ ] Cultural/myth references are researched and integrated respectfully.
[ ] Premium value comes from craft, specificity, and close-up reward.
[ ] No pay-to-win, pay-to-lose, or pay-to-confuse behavior.
[ ] Skin reads on multiple maps and lighting conditions.
```
